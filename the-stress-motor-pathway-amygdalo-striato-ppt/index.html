🧠 The Stress-Motor Pathway (Amygdalo-Striato-PPT)

This pathway describes the neuroanatomical and biochemical sequence from sensory threat detection to motor system potentiation, via cortisol-driven glutamatergic upregulation in the striatum. It links emotional stress, habit biasing, and motor readiness, and plays a central role in disorders like ALS, REM Sleep Behavior Disorder (RBD), and Fibromyalgia (FM).

1. Sensory Input → Thalamus → Amygdala

External stimuli are relayed via the thalamus:

  • LGN for visual input

  • MGN for auditory input

  • Ventrobasal complex for somatosensory input

These project rapidly to the Basolateral Amygdala (BLA).

2. Amygdala → Hypothalamus → HPA Axis Activation → Cortisol Priming of the Striatum

The Central Amygdala (CeA) indirectly activates the PVN via a disinhibition circuit:

  • CeA inhibits BST (both are GABAergic)

  • This disinhibits the PVN, initiating the stress hormone cascade

The PVN releases CRH, which prompts the pituitary to secrete ACTH, causing the adrenal cortex to release cortisol.

3. Cortisol → Striatal Modulation (Dorsolateral Striatum)

Cortisol binds to glucocorticoid receptors (GRs) in the Dorsolateral Striatum (DLS).

  • D1-MSNs become more excitable to glutamate

  • Inhibition by SOM+ interneurons is reduced

  • Net result: increased output, increased risk of excitotoxicity

4. Striatum → Basal Ganglia Output → PPT Disinhibition

D1-MSNs project directly to:

  • SNr

  • GPi

This reduces basal ganglia inhibition, disinhibiting the Pedunculopontine Tegmental Nucleus (PPT).

5. PPT → Reticulospinal Tract → Motor System

PPT activates reticulospinal neurons, leading to:

  • Increased muscle tone

  • Increased postural reflexes (PPA)

  • Increased startle reactivity

6. Chronic Activation → Excitotoxic Risk

Chronic activation leads to glutamate overload and motor neuron degeneration.
